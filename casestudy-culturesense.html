Culture Sense — Redesigning Cultural Transformation Monitoring at Novartis

A complete UX redesign that took user satisfaction from 1.5/5 to 4/5 — fixing WCAG compliance, information architecture, and visual overload.

Client Novartis
My Role UX Lead
Timeline Q1 2024 – Q4 2025
Team 2 FE · 2 Qlik · 2 Architects · 2 Data Scientists · 10k+ Users
Design System OneDS · 6 months
Culture Sense

Product to monitor & Identify cultural transformation within Novartis

Overview

The Challenge

Culture Sense is a product built for Novartis to help managers and executive committee members monitor and identify cultural transformation across the organisation. When we inherited it, the product was scoring 1.5 out of 5 in user satisfaction — not because the underlying data wasn't valuable, but because the interface made it nearly impossible to use.

Long scrolls, too many colours, WCAG contrast failures, cramped filters, and information overload — all five issues showing up simultaneously in a product that executives needed to trust.

The redesign goal was clear: make the product simple, intuitive, and accessible — WCAG compliant, with a reduced colour palette, a restructured information architecture, and filters that scale without breaking. Serving 10,000+ users across managers and executive committee members, the stakes for getting this right were high.

Product

Who Uses It & How

Culture Sense serves three primary user groups — Managers, Executive Committee members, and Novartis leadership. Each has a distinct mental model for what "cultural health" means, which directly shaped how we structured the information hierarchy.

The CAI (Culture Adoption Index) tracks four culture pillars — Inspired, Curious, Unbossed, and Integrity — across regions and job levels. A manager's typical session: land on Global Adoption, check their region's pillar breakdown, spot what's lagging, drill into Org Factors for context, then plan an action.

Research

Five Problems, One Product

User satisfaction was at 1.5/5 when we ran the initial assessment. Desk research and user testing sessions surfaced five distinct issues — each one serious on its own, all of them compounding each other in practice.

📜
Long scrolls leading to poor page performance. The entire CAI page was a single continuous view — Global Adoption, Culture Pillars, Adoption Trend, and all Org Factors stacked vertically. Page load suffered, and users lost orientation mid-scroll.
🎨
Colour contrast failing WCAG compliance. Orange and amber bars on a dark blue background — the primary data visualisation pattern — failed contrast ratio requirements. Critical information was inaccessible to users with visual impairments.
🌈
Too many colours in use. Four different chart colours for the four pillars, plus separate palettes for map states, trend lines, and UI components. No systematic colour language meant users couldn't build mental models for what colours meant.
🗜
Filter section cramping for space. When multiple filters were active, the filter row overflowed — chips wrapped onto multiple lines, pushing content down and obscuring the breadcrumb. Active state became visually noisy and hard to manage.
📦
Too much information shown at once. All data levels were visible simultaneously — global summary, regional breakdown, pillar details, and org factors — without progressive disclosure. Users didn't know where to look first.
User Stories

What Users Actually Needed

From the research synthesis, two user stories emerged that shaped all design decisions that followed.

Story 1 — Performance

As a user, I would like to view details without longer scroll, so that I don't experience delay caused by page load.

Story 2 — Accessibility

As a user, I would like to view an application that's simple, intuitive, and accessible with legible colours used, so that it can help in understanding the information and formulate strategy accordingly.

Information Architecture

Restructuring What Was There

Before proposing any visual changes, we interrogated the existing IA. Why was all information stacked? Was it a business requirement or a technical default? Was it driven by a user need we hadn't understood yet?

The existing structure had Global Adoption, Overall Trend for CA, Culture Pillars, and five different Org Factor sub-sections all living on a single page. The proposed restructure separated Org Factors and Demographic Factors into a navigable section, reducing the primary view to three focused components — Global Adoption, Culture Pillars, and Adoption Trend.

Design Exploration

Three Approaches, Two Tested

We explored three distinct approaches before moving into high fidelity. Approaches 1 and 2 were taken all the way to resonance testing with real users.

Approach 1
Card-based Exploration

CAI summary at top, Org Factors surfaced as cards below with Explore CTAs. Clean separation of levels. Filter panel on left sidebar.

Approach 2
KPI-driven Sidebar

Left panel shows all KPI scores in a scannable list. Right panel shows the selected detail. Inline navigation, no page transitions needed.

Approach 3
Personalised Country View

Countries categorised by CAI score. Map-first entry point. Pillar breakdown and trend per selected country on drill-down.

Resonance Testing

What Users Said

We ran resonance testing with real users on Version 1 and Version 2 high-fidelity designs. The feedback was revealing — and sometimes contradictory between users, which pushed us toward a synthesis rather than a single winner.

Version 1 — Card-based

✓ Liked

"It's very, very clear, very intuitive. Overall impression is good."

✓ Liked

"I think the other one has the advantage of having more clarity. When you look at the page you just see one thing."

✗ Disliked

"Each time, if I have to explore details related to org factors, I will have to click the card and explore."

✓ Liked

"I think if you're going to go to this type of approach, this one may make more sense to people."

Version 2 — KPI sidebar

✓ Liked

"I like this because it instils curiosity immediately. To understand this better I would deep dive on it."

✓ Liked

"I like always to look at many things in parallel to do my own cross-referencing. So having the numbers on the left, I like it."

✗ Disliked

"I find this too busy. So it's overwhelming because I don't know where I'm looking at first."

✓ Liked

"I like mostly the view where I can see all details in one screen. I don't need to consider the scroll."

Visual Design

Final High-Fidelity Solution

The final solution synthesised the clarity of Version 1 with the comprehensiveness of Version 2. Level 1 information — Global Adoption and Culture Pillars — is displayed immediately. The KPI sidebar from Version 2 provides quick navigation without overwhelming the primary view. Filters are distinct, scalable, and no longer wrap destructively.

Impacts

From 1.5 to 4 — Every Issue Resolved

The redesign addressed every one of the five issues identified at the start. User satisfaction moved from 1.5/5 to 4/5 — a significant jump for a product serving executive-level users where trust in the data is inseparable from trust in the interface presenting it.

4/5
User satisfaction rating after redesign — up from 1.5/5
5✓
Critical issues fully resolved — scroll, contrast, colour, filters, information overload
10k+
End users impacted across Novartis managers and executive committee members

The most tangible change was the page structure. By separating Org Factors and Demographic Factors into a navigable sub-section, page load improved substantially. By reducing the colour palette to a system built around orange-amber-cream for culture data and teal for benchmarks, WCAG compliance was met while actually making the charts easier to read. Filters became a single inline row with a "View More" overflow — expandable on demand, invisible when not needed.

📜
Scroll & page performance resolved

Reorganised IA moved Org Factors and Demographic Factors into a separate navigable section, reducing the primary page length by ~60% and eliminating the main cause of render lag.

♿
WCAG compliance achieved

Colour palette rebuilt from scratch against WCAG 2.1 AA contrast ratios. Orange (#C85C00 on white) and amber (#A86800 on white) both meet 4.5:1. All text labels confirmed accessible.

🎨
Reduced to a coherent colour language

From 8+ ad-hoc colours to a systematic three-level palette — High/Medium/Low mapped consistently across the entire product. Users can now build intuition: dark orange means high adoption, light cream means low.

🗜
Filter section made scalable

Active filter chips moved to a single collapsible inline row. Quarter and active selections visible at a glance. "See less / View more" pattern handles overflow without layout breakage at any filter count.

📊
Progressive disclosure replaced information overload

Level 1 information — global map, culture pillars, adoption trend — displayed immediately. Org Factors and Demographic Factors accessible on demand via navigation. Executives see the summary first, analysts can drill instantly.

Reflection

What I Learned

The most instructive part of this project was the resonance testing. Both design approaches received positive signals — but for completely different reasons. Version 1 was praised for clarity; Version 2 for comprehensiveness. The final solution synthesised both, which taught me that user testing rarely gives you a single answer. It gives you a map of trade-offs.

The IA restructure was more impactful than any visual change. Moving Org Factors and Demographic Factors from a flat long scroll into a navigable section — informed by data about what users actually accessed — reduced page load times and cognitive load simultaneously. Structure is design.

If I were to do this again, I'd run resonance testing even earlier — before high fidelity. The insight that Version 2 felt "too busy" to some while feeling "exactly right" to power users came too late to fully explore a third path in the time we had.
